The Pursuit of True Decentralization in Social Media: Failed Attempts

Author: LV (mariavasq@proton.me)

Published: April 20, 2025

Abstract

Despite the proliferation of blockchain technologies and decentralization rhetoric, most social media platforms remain fundamentally centralized. This paper explores why previous decentralization efforts failed, what philosophical commitments are necessary, and how stateless, user-governed systems might succeed where others collapsed. It includes a firsthand case study of Project X and proposes a governance model rooted in self-filtering, on-chain voting, and zero founder control.

1. Introduction: Centralized Control in Disguise

Social media governance today is predominantly controlled by algorithms, corporations, and moderators—centralized entities wielding significant influence over speech, reach, and digital identity. While some platforms profess decentralization, they often fall short of the ideal: they may be open-source or employ blockchain technology, but governance structures remain embedded within the founding team or a foundation that retains final authority.

True decentralization transcends mere technical architecture; it represents a philosophical paradigm shift in the governance of online communities.

2. What Is True Decentralization?

A decentralized social media platform must adhere to the following criteria:

  • Absence of Central Authority: No single entity should possess the power to moderate, promote, or suppress content.
  • User-Governed Filtering: Users must have complete control over the content they encounter or engage with.
  • Blockchain Governance: Censorship, promotion, and reputation mechanisms should be determined through smart contracts and on-chain voting.
  • Self-Regulation Through Community: Harmful or unwanted content should be filtered out not through top-down moderation, but through democratic tools like staking and flagging.

3. The Myth of Decentralized Moderation

Existing platforms fail for several reasons:

  • Founders’ Control: DAOs often retain central authority via token allocations or development rights.
  • Backend Gatekeeping: Key infrastructure remains under centralized control.
  • Lawlessness vs. Liability: Projects often revert to central moderation due to legal and ethical obligations.

4. A Personal Lesson: Why Project X Failed

In 2015, I launched Project X Open Source. It claimed to be decentralized, but I held the power to silence users and remove content. It wasn't truly decentralized—it was mine. That contradiction eroded my integrity, so I shut it down. Not because it didn’t work—but because it was a lie.

Over time, I realized that a truly decentralized platform is not something you own; it's something you release.

5. Governance by the People

Governance must emerge from users:

  • Reputation-Weighted Staking: Users vote with stake and reputation.
  • Custom Filters: Everyone filters their own experience, not others'.
  • Forkable Identity: Users must be able to fork the platform and take their identity with them.

6. Control vs. Freedom

Decentralization requires founders to surrender power. It invites friction, disagreement, and even chaos. But out of that chaos comes true user governance. It’s not about building a perfect system—it’s about letting the community shape it.

7. No Founders. No Investors.

A truly decentralized platform cannot have founders or investors with controlling stakes. Ownership is control. And control kills decentralization. If any person or entity can override community decisions, the system is not decentralized—it’s a puppet show.

8. Conclusion: Stateless Creation

A genuinely decentralized platform is a blank page. No top-down morality. No governance royalty. Just code, users, and community consensus. Founders must become equal participants. Investors must not exist. Only then can we escape the cycle of centralization.
